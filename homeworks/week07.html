Probability Theory: Interpretations, Axioms, and Measure Theory

Probability has several interpretations—classical, frequentist, Bayesian, geometric. The axiomatic approach unifies them, and measure theory supplies the rigorous foundation.

1. Interpretations of Probability

1.1 Classical

\[ P(A)=\frac{\text{favorable}}{\text{possible}}. \]

1.2 Frequentist

Probability = long-run frequency.

1.3 Bayesian

\[ P(H\mid D)=\frac{P(D\mid H)P(H)}{P(D)}. \]

1.4 Geometric

\[ P(A)=\frac{\text{measure}(A)}{\text{measure}(R)}. \]

1.5 Axioms Reconcile Them

All interpretations produce a function \(P\) obeying:

  • \(P(A)\ge 0\)
  • \(P(\Omega)=1\)
  • \(P(\bigcup_i A_i)=\sum_i P(A_i)\) for disjoint \(A_i\)

2. Probability and Measure Theory

2.1 Sigma-algebras

A σ-algebra \(\mathcal{F}\) is closed under complements and countable unions.

2.2 Probability Measures

A probability measure is a function \(P:\mathcal{F}\to[0,1]\) obeying the axioms.

2.3 Random Variables

A random variable is a measurable function \(X:\Omega\to\mathbb{R}\): \[ X^{-1}(B)\in\mathcal{F}. \] Its distribution is \[ P_X(B)=P(X\in B), \] and expectation is \[ \mathbb{E}[X]=\int_\Omega X\,dP. \]

3. Consequences of the Axioms

3.1 Subadditivity

For any events \(A_1,A_2,\dots\), \[ P\!\left(\bigcup_i A_i\right)\le \sum_i P(A_i). \]

Proof: Define disjoint sets \[ \begin{aligned} B_1 &= A_1,\\ B_2 &= A_2 \setminus A_1,\\ B_3 &= A_3 \setminus (A_1 \cup A_2),\\ &\;\vdots \\ B_n &= A_n \setminus \bigcup_{k=1}^{n-1} A_k. \end{aligned} \]

By construction: \[ B_i \subseteq A_i,\qquad B_i \text{ are disjoint} \] and \[ \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i = \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty B_i. \] Using countable additivity on the disjoint \(B_i\): \[ P\!\left(\bigcup_i A_i\right) = \sum_i P(B_i) \le \sum_i P(A_i). \]
